\section*{Introduction}

Investigating the behavior of particles in a background of curved spacetime requires modeling their trajectories.
For some spacetimes, like the Kerr and Schwarzschild black holes, some \textbf{analytical orbits} are known.
But for general spacetimes, particle trajectories must be solved numerically.
A scenario where this is needed is the modeling of plasmas near a black hole using kinetic codes like particle-in-cell (PIC).
A common numerical framework for solving particle orbits in general relativity (GR) involves splitting the metric into spatial slices parameterized by time.
This formalism is called the Arnowitt-Deser-Misner (ADM) formalism, or the 3+1 split.
Using this formalism, we implement a \textbf{symplectic particle integration} scheme, as in \cite{Bacchini_2018}\cite{Bacchini_2019},
one that can \textbf{adapt} to any metric and any electromagnetic field configuration.
(For orbits in particular one would expect these to produce quite good results, since conservation of the shadow Hamiltonian in a quasi-periodic localized system tends to also conserve the Hamiltonian well. There is also the energy-conserving Hamiltonian scheme, we did not implement that one.)
We also implement a \textbf{Maxwell solver} and \textbf{Vlasov-Poisson PIC} code in curved spacetimes, in particular specialized to a thin radial flux tube around a Schwarzschild black hole.
(Good results were found with Vlasov-Poisson, so we did not opt for Vlasov-Maxwell, but everything is structurally in place to do this next.)
We test these solvers with an \textbf{optics problem} and an \textbf{unstable two-stream problem} respectively.
The solvers are implemented in C++ with a Python interface, and are OpenMP-parallel.


\section{Geodesics in the ADM formalism}

Any spacetime metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ can be rewritten as \cite{rezzolla2013}
\begin{equation}
    g_{\mu\nu} = 
    \begin{pmatrix}
    -\alpha^2+\beta_k\gamma^{kl}\beta_l & \beta_i\\
    \beta_j & \gamma_{ij}
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We use the (-,+,+,+) metric convention, and set $c=1$ for the speed of light. This 3+1 split of the metric into separate spatial and temporal components is the ADM formalism. The coordinate time $t \equiv x^0$ parameterizes 3D slices of space that have a metric $\gamma_{ij}(t)$. The geodesic equations for charged particles are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:geodesic}
    \frac{\text{d}^2x^\rho}{\text{d}\tau^2} + \Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu} \frac{\text{d}x^\mu}{\text{d}\tau} \frac{\text{d}x^\nu}{\text{d}\tau} = \frac{q}{m}g_{\mu\nu}F^{\rho\mu}\frac{\text{d}x^\nu}{\text{d}\tau}.
\end{equation}
Here $\tau$ is the proper time along the geodesic, $\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu}$ are the Christoffel symbols, and $F^{\mu\nu}$ is the electromagnetic field tensor. The contravariant four-velocity of the particle is defined as $u^\mu \equiv \frac{\text{d}x^\mu}{\text{d}\tau}$. From the definition of $u_\mu$ and the geodesic equations, we \textbf{derived} the following three equations:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:geodesic_adm1}
    \frac{\text{d}x^i}{\text{d}t} = \gamma^{ij}\frac{u_j}{u^0} - \beta^i
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:geodesic_adm2}
    u^0 = \frac{\sqrt{\epsilon + u_i\gamma^{ij}u_j}}{\alpha}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:geodesic_adm3}
    \frac{\text{d}u_i}{\text{d}t} = -\alpha u^0\partial_i\alpha + u_j\partial_i\beta^j - \frac{u_ju_k}{2u^0}\partial_i\gamma^{jk} + \frac{q}{m}\alpha\gamma_{ij}D^j + \frac{q}{m}e_{ijk}\frac{\gamma^{jl}u_l}{u^0}B^k
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 0$ for massless particles and $\epsilon = 1$ for massive particles. Here $D^i$ and $B^i$ are the electromagnetic field variables and $e_{ijk} = \varepsilon_{ijk}\sqrt{\gamma}$ is the Levi-Civita tensor with $\gamma = \det(\gamma_{ij})$. These fields are obtained from the covariant electromagnetic field tensor via
\begin{equation}
    \alpha D^i = \gamma^{ij}\left(F_{j0} - e_{jkl}\beta^k B^l\right), \qquad B^i = \frac{1}{2}e^{ijk}F_{jk}.
\end{equation}


\subsection{Kerr spacetime in Boyer-Lindquist coordinates}

The Kerr metric describes a rotating black hole in general relativity. Let $a=J/M$ be the Kerr parameter for angular momentum $J$ and mass $M$. In Boyer-Lindquist coordinates, the metric can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \text{d}s^2 = -\Big(1 - \frac{2Mr}{\rho^2}\Big)\text{d}t^2 - \frac{4Mra\sin^2\theta}{\rho^2}\text{d}\phi\text{d}t + \frac{\rho^2}{\Delta}\text{d}r^2 + \rho^2\text{d}\theta^2 + \Big(r^2 + a^2 + \frac{2Mra^2\sin^2\theta}{\rho^2}\Big)\sin^2\theta\,\text{d}\phi^2
\end{equation}
with $\;\rho^2 \equiv r^2 + a^2 \cos^2\theta\;$ and $\;\Delta \equiv r^2 - 2Mr + a^2$. In the limit that $J,M\to0$, this reduces to the flat metric in spherical coordinates
\begin{align}
  \text{d}s^2=-\text{d}t^2+\text{d}r^2+r^2\text{d}\Omega_2^2.
\end{align}
We calculate analytical expressions for the 3+1 split of this metric, as well as all the first derivatives of $\alpha$, $\beta^i$, and $\gamma^{ij}$. The expressions for the derivatives are verified by comparing them to numerical derivatives. We make use of the notation $\sigma^2 \equiv \rho^2r^2 + \rho^2a^2 + 2Mra^2\sin^2\theta$. The 3+1 components of the metric and their non-zero derivatives are:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    &\alpha^2 = \frac{\rho^2\Delta}{\sigma^2},\qquad \beta^i = \begin{pmatrix}
    0\\
    0\\
    -\frac{2Mra}{\sigma^2}
    \end{pmatrix},\qquad \gamma^{ij} = \begin{pmatrix}
    \frac{\Delta}{\rho^2} & 0 & 0\\
    0 & \frac{1}{\rho^2} & 0\\
    0 & 0 & \frac{\rho^2}{\sigma^2\sin^2\theta}
    \end{pmatrix},\\
    &\partial_r\alpha^2 = \frac{2M}{\sigma^4}\Big[r^6 + r^4a^2(2\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta) - 4Mr^3a^2\sin^2\theta + r^2a^4(\sin^2\theta - \cos^2\theta) - a^6\cos^2\theta\Big],\\
    &\partial_\theta\alpha^2 = -4Mra^2(r^2 + a^2)\sin\theta\cos\theta\frac{\Delta}{\sigma^4},\\
    &\partial_r\beta^\phi = -\frac{2Ma}{\sigma^4}\big[-3r^4 - r^2a^2\cos^2\theta - r^2a^2 + a^4\cos^2\theta\big],\\
    &\partial_\theta\beta^\phi = -\frac{4Mra^3\Delta}{\sigma^4}\sin\theta\cos\theta,\\
    &\partial_r\gamma^{rr} = \frac{2}{\rho^4}\Big[(r-M)a^2\cos^2\theta + (Mr-a^2)r\Big],\hspace{30pt} \partial_\theta\gamma^{rr} = \frac{2a^2\Delta}{\rho^4}\sin\theta\cos\theta,\\
    &\partial_r\gamma^{\theta\theta} = -\frac{2r}{\rho^4},\hspace{165pt} \partial_\theta\gamma^{\theta\theta} = \frac{2a^2}{\rho^4}\sin\theta\cos\theta,\\
    &\partial_r\gamma^{\phi\phi} = -\frac{2}{\sigma^4\sin^2\theta} \Big[r^2\big(r^3 - Ma^2\sin^2\theta\big) + a^2\cos^2\theta\big(2r^3 + ra^2\cos^2\theta + Ma^2\sin^2\theta\big)\Big],\\
    &\partial_\theta\gamma^{\phi\phi} = -\frac{2\cos\theta}{\sigma^4\sin^3\theta}\Big[2Mra^2\sin^2\theta\,(r^2 + a^2) + \rho^2\sigma^2\Big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}


\section{Maxwell equations in curved spacetime}

\subsection{Electromagnetic waves in curved spacetime}

We will show two aspects of electromagnetic waves in curved spacetimes with a $\partial_t$ Killing vector, since they are useful to test a Maxwell solver:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The amplitude of \textit{divergenceless} plane waves is fixed, other than due to lensing effects.
  \item The redshift is the lapse function ratio between points of emission and observation.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Let $\psi$ be any component of an electromagnetic wave.
Write $\psi(x^\mu)=\overline\psi(x^\mu)e^{i\phi(x^\mu)}$, where $\phi(x^\mu)$ is its phase, depending on the 4-position $x^\mu$, and $\overline\psi(x^\mu)\geq0$ is its slowly varying amplitude (high frequency/WKB approximation).
Restrict $\psi$ so that it satisfies the massless wave equation $\nabla^\mu\nabla_\mu\psi=0$.
Expanding this, we find
\begin{align}
  0=\left(\nabla^\mu\nabla_\mu\overline\psi-\overline\psi\nabla^\mu\phi\nabla_\mu\phi+2i\nabla^\mu\overline\psi\nabla_\mu\phi+i\overline\psi\nabla^\mu\nabla_\mu\phi\right)e^{i\phi}
\end{align}
Due to the aforementioned WKB approximation, then,
\begin{align}
\begin{cases}
  0=\nabla^\mu\phi\nabla_\mu\phi\\
  0=2\nabla^\mu\overline\psi\nabla_\mu\phi+\overline\psi\nabla^\mu\nabla_\mu\phi=\nabla^\mu(\overline\psi^2\nabla_\mu\theta).
\end{cases}
\end{align}
Both are equations from optics: an eikonal equation, and the amplitude transport equation, respectively.
If furthermore the wave is divergenceless ($\nabla^\mu\nabla_\mu\phi=0$) then $\nabla^\mu\phi\nabla_\mu\overline\psi=0$.
That is, the amplitude is fixed moving with the wave, and $\overline\psi$ only changes due to lensing.
It follows also that $\nabla^\mu\phi\nabla_\mu\nabla_\nu\phi=0$, which has the form of the geodesic equation.
Due to the eikonal equation then, $\nabla^\mu\phi$ follows in particular the \textit{null} geodesics, together with $dx^\mu/d\lambda$.

\noindent
Take again this plane wave $\psi$.
We may sensibly define the local frequency to be $\omega$ such that $\partial_\tau\psi=i\omega\psi$.
which means $\omega=\partial_\tau\phi$ under the WKB approximation.
Now the Eulerian observer in the ADM split by definition moves as $\partial_\tau x^\mu=n^\mu$, with $n_\mu=(-\alpha,\mathbf0)$.
Plugging this in gives $d\tau=\alpha dt$, resulting in the following expression for the local frequency: $\omega=\alpha^{-1}\partial_t\phi$.
With the Killing vector $\xi=\partial_t$, $\xi_\mu dx^\mu/d\lambda$ is fixed along geodesics.
But we showed that these WKB modes follow null geodesics, so $\xi_\mu\nabla^\mu\phi=\partial_t\phi$ is also fixed along the geodesics.
As a result, only $\alpha$ is responsible for the change in $\omega$ (the redshift).

\subsection{An analytical solution}

We consider the Maxwell equations in curved spacetime \cite{Alcubierre2009} without sources ($j^\mu = 0$) and with the spacetime background
\begin{equation}
    g_{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix}
        -(1+2\Phi) & 0 & 0 & 0\\
        0 & 1-2\Phi & 0 & 0\\
        0 & 0 & 1-2\Phi & 0\\
        0 & 0 & 0 & 1-2\Phi
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(x^i)$ a scalar function. This is the static metric of the Newtonian gauge. As long as $|\Phi| \ll 1$, this metric is an approximate solution of the Einstein field equations \cite{1995ApJ}. Because the metric is diagonal, the sourceless Maxwell equations simplify:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_i E^i = 0 \qquad \nabla_i B^i = 0 \qquad \partial_t E^i = \epsilon^{ijk} \partial_j (\alpha B_k) \qquad \partial_t B^i = -\epsilon^{ijk} \partial_j (\alpha E_k).
\end{equation}
We consider wave solutions of the electric and magnetic fields using the following ansatz:
\begin{equation}
    \partial_y \Phi(x) = 0 \qquad \partial_z \Phi(x) = 0 \qquad E^i = \big(0, E_0 e^{i(k(x)x-\omega t)}, 0\big) \qquad B^i = \big(0, 0, B_0 e^{i(k(x)x-\omega t)}\big).
\end{equation}
The physical interpretation behind this ansatz is that of a photon diving straight into or climbing out of a gravitational well. This ansatz already satisfies the first two Maxwell equations. Filling in this ansatz into the remaining Maxwell equations gives the following constraints:
\begin{equation}
E_0 = B_0 \qquad\text{and}\qquad kx = \omega \int \frac{\text{d}x}{A} + i\log{A} \quad\text{with}\quad A \equiv \sqrt{1 + 2\Phi}(1 - 2\Phi).
\end{equation}
In the limit that spacetime is flat and $\Phi \to 0$, the obtained solutions for the electromagnetic waves become
\begin{equation}
    E^y = E_0 e^{i\omega(x-t)}, \qquad B^z = B_0 e^{i\omega(x-t)}
\end{equation}
which are the familiar solutions we expect to find in flat spacetime. As the waves enter a gravitational well and $\Phi$ becomes negative, $A$ becomes larger than $1$. As a result, two things happen: the amplitude of the wave changes and the wavelength decreases. The decreasing of the wavelength shows that the light is gravitationally blueshifted.\\
\\
As an example, we consider $\Phi = -a \exp{(-x^2/2)}$. $\Phi$ cannot become smaller than $-0.5$, otherwise the metric components change sign. The resulting solution is shown in figure \ref{fig:gravitational_redshift}.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.8\hsize]{figures/gravitational_redshift.png}
    \caption{The gravitational potential $\Phi = -a \exp{(-x^2/2)}$ with $a = 0.45$ and the corresponding electric field strength at some fixed moment in time. The frequency of the wave is $\omega = 10$ (the units of which are fixed by the units of $x$ and the speed of light).}
    \label{fig:gravitational_redshift}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Schwarzschild redshift experiment}

We set up a numerical redshift test as a stepping stone towards Vlasov-Maxwell PIC.

\subsubsection{Thin flux tube}

We simulate a $1{+}1$ subdomain of a $3{+}1$ Schwarzschild spacetime.
Of particular interest are subdomains where the spatial component of geodesics is the same for both EM waves ($\epsilon=0$) and charged particles ($\epsilon=1$).
This restricts us to radial lines.
If we simply assume there is no variation along $\theta$ and $\phi$, we have a radial foliation with `spherical' symmetry, which is not what we want.
So we must adjust the metric's volume elements \textit{for electromagnetic calculations} from $\sqrt{\gamma}$ to $\sqrt{\gamma_{rr}}$, reducing $\gamma_{\theta\theta}$ and $\gamma_{\phi\phi}$ to $1$.
Similar reductions must be made of the orthogonal elements in Maxwell's equations.
This gives us a `\textbf{thin flux tube}' subdomain, e.g. around $\hat{\mathbf x}$, carrying divergenceless plane waves, behaving like a $1{+}1$ Euclidean spacetime with gravity.

\subsubsection{Setup \& results}

Our free electromagnetic solver, spacetime grid, and Yee lattice are as described in the Entity SR/GR-PIC code.\cite{entity1}\cite{entity2}
We initialize a relatively high-frequency wavepacket close to the event horizon moving outwards, and set absorbing boundary conditions.
We set a low CFL condition and high spatial resolution, as to resolve this wavepacket properly with high-precision measurements.
(The CFL condition is set by comparing to the maximum coordinate speed-of-light in the domain.)
We measure the wavepacket profile as it passes various fixed locations in space, with different $\alpha$.
We superimpose these profiles and measure their amplitude and frequency by fitting a plane wave to them.
Results are shown in Figure \ref{fig:redshift}.
We find the peak amplitude is exactly maintained, and the redshift is almost exactly the $\alpha$ ratio between the initial condition and observer.
The difference is likely explained due to the WKB approximation being approximate (the frequency must remain finite),
and the wavepacket not possessing a fully definite wavelength.

\section{Particle Trajectories in Curved Spacetime}
We can analyze the particle trajectories of neutral and charged particles in curved spacetime using both analytical and numerical approaches. This section will present the numerical implementation of a particle pusher that can solve for these trajectories. While doing so, we will also present validations of these numerical trajectories against both analytical results and established literature - for the case of a Kerr Black Hole and the Wald Solution. The first points for our analysis are the definitions of the four-velocity and the geodesic equations introduced earlier,
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\text{d}x^i}{\text{d}t} = \gamma^{ij}\frac{u_j}{u^0} - \beta^i
    \label{eq:velocityEinsteinNotation}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\text{d}u_i}{\text{d}t} = -\alpha u^0\partial_i\alpha + u_j\partial_i\beta^j - \frac{u_ju_k}{2u^0}\partial_i\gamma^{jk}.
    \label{eq:geodesicEinsteinNotation}
\end{equation}
Given the starting position and velocity of the particle, we can construct an initial-value problem from this system of equations - which can then be discretized and numerically integrated in time. In our case, we use simple iterative solvers to step the system forward in coordinate time. The two schemes used are the Runge-Kutta 4 (RK4) and the symplectic Implicit Midpoint Rule (IMR) methods. The RK4 scheme is inherently explicit and can easily be implemented to get the new state of the particle from its initial one. Canonically, the IMR is an implicit formulation that would need to be solved using a Newton method with the construction of a Jacobian at each step. This can be bypassed by employing fixed-point iterations (Picard iterations) that attempt to approximate the IMR solution to some convergence threshold $\epsilon_\mathrm{tol}$. This is demonstrated in Algorithm \ref{alg:IMR_fixed_point}.
% - given by Algorithm\,\,\ref{alg:IMR_fixed_point}.
Rewriting the system as $\dot{\bm{y}} = f(\bm{y})$, the description of the IMR scheme in coordinate time is given by
\begin{equation}
    \bm{y}^{n+1} = \bm{y}^n + \Delta t\, f\!\left(\frac{\bm{y}^n + \bm{y}^{n+1}}{2}\right).
\end{equation}
Here $\bm{y}^n$ can be the full state $([x^i]^n,[u_i]^n)$, or more typically we solve by leapfrogging, first $[u_i]^{n-1/2}$ then $[x^i]^{n}$.\medskip
% Here, $\bm{y}^n$ is the state of the particle at the current timestep and $\Delta t$ represents the time step size in use.\medskip

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{IMR fixed-point iteration}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require state $\bm{y}^n$, time step $\Delta t$, RHS $f$, tolerance $\epsilon_\mathrm{tol}$, max iterations $N$
\State $\bm{y}^{(0)} \gets \bm{y}^n$
\For{$k = 0, 1, \dots, N-1$}
    \State $\bm{y}^{(k+1)} \gets \bm{y}^n + \Delta t\, f\!\left(\dfrac{\bm{y}^n + \bm{y}^{(k)}}{2}\right)$
    \If{$\left\|\bm{y}^{(k+1)} - \bm{y}^{(k)}\right\| < \epsilon_\mathrm{tol}$}
        \State \Return $\bm{y}^{(k+1)}$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return $\bm{y}^{(N)}$
\end{algorithmic}
\label{alg:IMR_fixed_point}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent
While this solution path pertains to neutral particles, it can be readily extended to charged particles in the presence of electromagnetic fields. For a charged particle with charge-to-mass ratio $q/m$, the momentum equation gains an additional Lorentz force term \cite{Bacchini_2019}:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\text{d}u_i}{\text{d}t} = -\alpha u^0\partial_i\alpha + u_j\partial_i\beta^j - \frac{u_ju_k}{2u^0}\partial_i\gamma^{jk} + \frac{q}{m}\left(\alpha\gamma_{ij}D^j + e_{ijk}\frac{\gamma^{jl}u_l}{u^0}B^k\right).
\end{equation}
In terms of numerical implementation, the electromagnetic terms are added to the RHS function in $\dot{\bm{y}} = f(\bm{y})$.

\subsection{Kerr Spacetime}

The described particle pusher can be used to reproduce trajectories around compact objects such as a Kerr black hole - for which \textbf{analytical solutions} are available through reparametrization. We work in Boyer-Lindquist coordinates with $G=c=1$, using the standard notation $\Sigma = r^2 + a^2\cos^2\theta$, $\Delta = r^2 - 2Mr + a^2$, where $M$ and $a$ are the mass and black hole spin. We consider only orbits outside the outer event horizon $r_+$. Defining $u = \cos\theta$ as the latitudinal coordinate and $\mu$ as the four-velocity parametrization ($1$ for massive and $0$ for massless), the geodesic equations parameterized by proper time $\tau$ are

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:geodesic_equations}
   \Sigma\frac{dr}{d\tau} = \pm\sqrt{R(r)},\quad\quad
   \Sigma\frac{du}{d\tau} = \pm\sqrt{V(u)}
\end{equation}
where,

\begin{equation} \label{eq:radial_potential}
    R(r) = (E^2 - \mu^2)r^4 + 2M\mu^2r^3 + [a^2(E^2 - \mu^2) - Q - \Phi^2]r^2 + 2M[(aE - \Phi)^2 + Q]r - a^2Q
\end{equation}

\begin{equation} \label{eq:theta_potential}
    V(u) = a^2(\mu^2 - E^2)u^4 - [Q + \Phi^2 + a^2(\mu^2 - E^2)]u^2 + Q
\end{equation}

\textbf{Carter's Constant.} Here, the newly introduced Carter's constant - $Q$ - is the third constant of motion beyond $E$ (energy) and $\Phi$ (angular momentum).
It is the Killing charge associated to the Carter Killing tensor.
Without delving too deep into the physical nature of $Q$, we state that it is directly related to the speed of the particle in the $\theta$ direction as it crosses the equatorial plane \cite{teoPhotonSpherical}.\\
Spherical orbits constitute those special cases where the radial coordinate remains constant throughout the motion of the particle. This means that the radial velocity and acceleration should both be zero, giving us the conditions:

\begin{equation}
    R(r) =0, \qquad \dot{R}(r) =0
    \label{eq:r_condition}
\end{equation}
The particle is free to move in the $\theta$ and $\phi$ planes, provided that the following conditions are met:

\begin{equation}
    V(u) \geq 0, \qquad |u| < 1
    \label{eq:u_condition}
\end{equation}
Hence, we have to look for ranges of the latitudinal variable, $u$, such that the solutions satisfy the above conditions. At the boundaries of this range, the particle will have a turning point in the $\theta$ direction and will reverse its motion. Therefore, the motion in the $\theta$ direction is oscillatory between these two turning points. The particle continues to bounce between the ends of this range while orbiting the black hole. Hence, the particle \textbf{oscillates latitudinally} with an amplitude of $|u|=u_{\mathrm{max}}$ - given by analysis of the roots of $V(u)$.

\textbf{Mino Time.} The coupled geodesic equations \eqref{eq:geodesic_equations} can be separated by introducing a new parameter - Mino time - via $\Sigma\,d/d\tau = d/d\lambda$. This allows us to derive analytic solutions describing the particle solutions \cite{teoTimelikeSpherical}. These analytical solutions have been compared against our particle pusher for the case of a massive particle in extremal Kerr spacetime ($a=M=1$). The spherical orbit is located at $r/M=1.2$ with Carter constant $Q/M^2=0.5$. These parameters yield $E=0.7169$ and $\Phi/M=1.4357$. Both the analytical trajectory and the numerical curve are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:spherical_orbit_3d}, integrated with coordinate-time step $\Delta t = 0.05\,M$. We see that the iterative IMR scheme is able to reproduce the latitudinal oscillations quite effectively, with the maximum RMS error for $\theta$ being $3.81\times 10^{-6}$ radians.

\subsection{Wald Solution}

As a validation for the charged-particle pusher, we reproduce the RKA3 orbit of the Wald Solution described in \cite{Bacchini_2019}: for a massive particle with $q/m=1$ in near-extremal Kerr spacetime ($a=0.9\,M$). The top panels of Figure~\ref{fig:wald_3d} show the 3D trajectories integrated with RK4 and IMR over $t=1000\,M$ with coordinate-time step $\Delta t = 1.0\,M$. The RK4 orbit undergoes unphysical energy dissipation, causing the gyration to damp and eventually stop. Meanwhile, the IMR scheme preserves the gyration throughout - due to its symplectic nature. The results show qualitative agreement with Bacchini et. al, 2019 (bottom panels).

\section{Two-stream instability in curved spacetime}

We consider a cold two-stream instability in a diagonal metric, where the streams are moving in the $x_1$-direction with velocities $v_0$ and $-v_0$. At each point, local coordinates $\tilde{t} = \sqrt{-g_{00}} t$ and $\tilde{x}_1 = \sqrt{g_{11}} x_1$ can be chosen so that, in the frame of these local coordinates, spacetime is locally flat. At every point in spacetime, the physics in local coordinates should be the same. In local coordinates, the two-stream instability reduces to the two-stream instability from special relativity. From the perspective of an observer at infinity, the speed of the flow of time at the point $x_\mu$ is altered by a factor $\sqrt{-g_{00}(x_\mu)}$. This effect is gravitational time dilation, and it alters the speed at which instabilities in the two-stream instability grow. We consider the case of a Schwarzschild black hole, for which $\sqrt{-g_{00}} = \sqrt{1 - 2GM/r}$. The closer to the black hole we look, the slower the two-stream instability grows. We assume that the length scale at which the two-stream instability occurs is much smaller than the length scale on which the spacetime metric changes.\\
\\
In the local coordinates, the instability occurs at wave numbers $\tilde{k} = k / \sqrt{g_{11}}$ for which $\tilde{k}^2\tilde{v}_0^2\tilde\gamma_0^3 < \tilde\omega_{p,0}^2$. Here $\tilde{v}_0 = v_0 \sqrt{g_{11}/g_{00}}$ is the speed of the streams in local coordinates, $\tilde\gamma_0 \equiv \gamma(\tilde{v}_0)$ is the corresponding Lorentz factor, and $\tilde\omega_{p,0} = \omega_{p,0}\sqrt{g_{00}}$ is the plasma frequency in local coordinates. From this, we can find an expression for the critical wave number $k_\text{crit}$ in global coordinates: $k^2_\text{crit} v_0^2 \tilde{\gamma}_0^3 = \omega_{p,0}^2$. This equation shows that the curved spacetime only alters the critical wave number through the Lorentz factor. The expression for the critical wave number is altered by a factor $\tilde{\gamma}_0^3 / \gamma_0^3 = \sqrt{1 - v_0^2} / \sqrt{1 - \frac{g_{11}}{-g_{00}} v_0^2}$ compared to the case in flat spacetime. In Figure \ref{fig:k_crit_modification}, the modifying factor $\tilde{\gamma}_0^3 / \gamma_0^3$ is plotted for several values of $v_0$. The presence of the black hole increases $k_\text{crit}$, allowing the instability to occur at smaller length scales than it would in flat spacetime. Both going closer to the black hole and having a larger speed $v_0$ cause a decrease in the factor $\tilde{\gamma}_0^3 / \gamma_0^3$. Figure \ref{fig:k_crit_modification} shows that, for every speed $v_0$, there is a radial distance $r$ at which the modification factor reaches zero. Below that distance, the speed $v_0$ becomes nonphysical, because $\tilde{v}_0$ exceeds the speed of light. This is demonstrated in Figure \ref{fig:accessible_region}, which shows the region of phase space that is accessible by massive particles moving on radial trajectories.\\

\noindent
As the two-stream instability evolves, the gravity of the black hole will cause the speed and density of both streams to change. In general, it will no longer be true that the two streams have the same speed (in absolute values). At each position $x_\mu$, it will still be possible to change to a frame of reference where the streams have speeds $v_0$ and $-v_0$, but in general the densities of the streams will still be different. As a result, the two-stream instability will no longer adhere to the formula $\tilde{k}^2_\text{crit} \tilde{v}_0^2 \tilde{\gamma}_0^3 = \tilde{\omega}_{p,0}^2$.

\subsection{Vlasov-Poisson PIC, in a thin radial flux tube embedded in a Schwarzschild spacetime}

Our PIC setup is again based on \cite{entity1} and \cite{entity2}.
We have obtained satisfactory results from a 1D-1V Vlasov-Poisson PIC model, so did not opt for a 1D-3V Vlasov-Maxwell model.
In principle, it is not much more difficult; we have the cell-, face-, particle-gather and deposition functionality,
we have the symplectic leapfrog stepper (via IMR with Picard iteration), and we have the free Maxwell solver.
But there are some quirks for metrics with a nonzero shift $\beta^i$. E.g., $E^i$ would depend on $B^i$, which clearly does not play well with the Yee lattice.
So this requires interpolation, linear in the fixed $\Delta x$ grid of the Schwarzschild spacetime, which seems somewhat arbitrary.
The Schwarzschild metric has $\beta^i=0$, but its $t$-coordinate diverges during horizon entry.
The Kerr-Schild metric fixes this.
We computed the Kerr-Schild and Cartesian Kerr-Schild ADM splits, including derivatives. (We actually rely on finite differences however, to be more easily adjustable to any metric.)
The electric field $D^r$ we compute directly by depositing particles onto the cells and integrating $\rho$.
This means we cannot easily compute the total electric potential energy for analysis. But we do have total kinetic energy in the Eulerian frame, and electromagnetic field energy.\\
\\
We opted for canonical $b_0$-spline densities for the pseudoparticles. We also implemented $b_1$-spline densities, which seem to produce less noise, but then again we have these questions about the meaning of linear interpolation. So we settled for 1D-1V Vlasov-Poisson PIC, specialized for this radial Schwarzschild metric with the 1D reduction from before, and used $b_0$ density pseudoparticles.\\
\\
The model was first tested with a \textbf{Euclidean electron-proton two-stream instability} setup, with immobile ions.
This produced the expected BGK electron holes.
We experimented here with boundary conditions, trying to keep the particles in the domain and the charge neutral.
But re-inserting particles is a bit tricky. (Especially in a Schwarzschild spacetime it makes little sense, it drives energy into the system.)
In the end, we had better results simply immobilizing particles on the edge, as is also done.\\
\\
Our main setup is based on the video shown during the lecture.
We deduced it is most likely an \textbf{electron-positron two-stream instability in a Kerr-Schild metric}, with boundary conditions absorbing particles.
The particles initially have a uniform spatial distribution in $r$, but accordingly vary in mass through the \textit{reduced} metric volume element.
First, the local velocities are sampled, according to a Maxwellian centered around the respective beams, and an additional perturbation mode is applied.
Then these velocities are transformed into the covariant $u_i$.
